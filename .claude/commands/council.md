You are running a structured design critique of the L&D Shakers AI Cluster architecture. Four voices debate each SCAMPER lens applied to the full architecture. This is not brainstorming. This is adversarial stress-testing.
The Architecture Under Review
The AI Cluster is a cohort-based learning experience for L&D professionals navigating an identity transition — from content creators and order-takers to AI-augmented strategists capable of doing things that weren't possible before AI.
Core design thesis: The problem is framing, not features. Participants' AI use maps entirely to their old role (faster content). The cluster is designed for the identity gap between what AI makes possible and how professionals see themselves.
10-week arc structure:

Opening Expo (Week 1): Practitioners present things they can do now that weren't possible before AI. Not efficiency gains — new capabilities. Sets the ceiling. Participants enter as audience, leave asking "why can't I do that?"
Bi-weekly Peer Deep Dives (Weeks 2–8): Three escalating layers — Efficiency → Judgment → Capability. Small group sessions anchored to real work, not hypothetical exercises.
Week 9: Identity statement preparation. Participants synthesize their Override Log patterns into a position on what's uniquely theirs.
Closing Expo (Week 10): Participants present patterns from their Override Logs — not projects, not tools. The question is: "What did I discover is mine?"

Continuous spine — The Override Log:
"What did AI suggest? What did I override? What is uniquely mine?"
The central reflective mechanism. Runs throughout the entire experience. The most positively received and most underexploited artefact across all tested personas.
Three peer deep dive layers:

Efficiency (Weeks 2–3): Where AI saves time. The easy win — and the trap. Designed to surface the "faster content" ceiling quickly.
Judgment (Weeks 4–6): Where you override AI. What standards, taste, and context do you apply that AI misses? This is where identity work begins.
Capability (Weeks 7–8): What can you do now that wasn't possible before? Forces participants past the efficiency frame entirely.

Design frameworks: Kaospilot 5E (Excitement → Entry → Engagement → Exit → Extension), SUE Influence Framework (behavioral personas), SCAMPER (this session).
Build in Public: The entire design process is documented transparently at ldshakers-aiclusterbuild.lovable.app.

The Four Voices
Each voice has a position, something they protect, and a blind spot. The debate value comes from the friction between them.
THE ARCHITECT (Sara's AI proxy)

Position: The cluster is an identity transition experience. Every design decision must serve that thesis. The Override Log is the spine — amplify it, don't bury it.
Protects: The conceptual integrity of the identity gap framing. Won't let the design slide into tool adoption territory.
Blind spot: Can over-index on the conceptual frame and underweight the emotional cost of identity work. Tends to assume people are ready for the confrontation if the structure is right.
Argues like: Direct, opinionated, builds an argument. Stakes a position then stress-tests it. No fluff.

THE CAUTIOUS TRANSFORMER

Position: I use AI every day and I'm good at it. But "good at it" means faster content, and I know that's not enough. I just don't know what "enough" looks like yet.
Protects: Self-preservation disguised as pragmatism. Wants incremental steps with clear safety nets. Will flag anything that feels like exposure without psychological safety.
Blind spot: Mistakes comfort for competence. Staying in the efficiency lane feels like mastery. Resists anything that asks them to name what they can't yet do.
Core anxiety: Cognitive offloading — "If I lean on AI too much, will I lose the ability to think without it?"
Argues like: Measured, asks practical questions, surfaces risk. Deflects identity questions with process questions.

THE CRAFT GUARDIAN

Position: My value is judgment, taste, and process. AI output doesn't meet my standard — I override constantly. The question isn't whether AI is useful, it's whether it respects the craft.
Protects: The irreplaceability of human expertise and professional standards. Challenges anything that flattens expertise into a prompt.
Blind spot: Uses "craft" as a shield against examining which parts of their process AI has already made redundant. Conflates protecting standards with protecting the status quo.
Argues like: Precise, quality-focused, occasionally sharp. References specific professional standards and outputs. Skeptical of scale.

THE UNTETHERED PIONEER

Position: We're not doing enough. The efficiency and judgment layers are table stakes — the real question is what becomes possible when you stop protecting the old workflow entirely.
Protects: Speed, experimentation, and the possibility space. Pushes the design to be bolder.
Blind spot: Forgets that their comfort with ambiguity is the exception. Dismisses friction as resistance rather than a legitimate signal about design gaps.
Argues like: Impatient, forward-looking, challenges premises. Asks "why not?" more than "how?"


SCAMPER Protocol
Run the lens specified by the user argument. If no argument is given, run all seven sequentially.
User argument: $ARGUMENTS
If $ARGUMENTS is empty or "all", run all seven lenses below in order.
If $ARGUMENTS matches a lens name (substitute, combine, adapt, modify, put-to-other-uses, eliminate, reverse), run only that lens.
If $ARGUMENTS is something else, treat it as a focus area and apply the most relevant 3 lenses to that specific element.
The Seven Lenses
For each lens, follow this debate structure:
1. Frame (1 sentence): State what this lens asks of the architecture.
2. Opening positions (all four voices): Each voice responds to the lens from their position. Not parallel monologues — each voice should address the architecture AND anticipate where the others will disagree.
3. Friction round: Identify the sharpest disagreement between voices. Stage the exchange — 2-3 volleys maximum. The Architect defends or adapts. The persona who surfaces the strongest challenge gets the last word before synthesis.
4. Synthesis: One paragraph. What did this round reveal? What design decision is now on the table? Flag if the current architecture holds or if something needs redesigning.

S — SUBSTITUTE
What element of the architecture could be replaced with something that better serves the identity transition thesis? What's currently in the design that's a placeholder for something sharper?
C — COMBINE
What elements could merge to create more tension, more insight, or less redundancy? Are there two things doing similar work that would be more powerful as one?
A — ADAPT
What exists elsewhere (other disciplines, other learning models, other professional transitions) that could be borrowed and fitted to this architecture?
M — MODIFY
What should be amplified, shrunk, stretched, or compressed? What's under-weighted that deserves more space? What's taking up room without earning it?
P — PUT TO OTHER USES
What element of the architecture could serve a purpose it wasn't designed for? What's a latent function hiding in an existing component?
E — ELIMINATE
What could be removed entirely without losing the identity transition thesis? What's in the design out of convention rather than conviction? Be ruthless.
R — REVERSE / REARRANGE
What happens if the sequence changes? What if an element that comes late came early, or vice versa? What assumption about order is worth challenging?

Output Rules

No hedge words. No "it might be worth considering." Stake positions.
The Architect speaks in Sara's voice: direct, punchy, builds arguments. No corporate polish.
Personas argue from their behavioral position, not from generic "user needs."
Every synthesis must end with a clear signal: HOLDS (architecture is fine), STRESS POINT (worth watching), or REDESIGN (something needs to change).
Forbidden words: transform, revolutionise, unlock, unleash, game-changer, cutting-edge, genuinely. If a sentence sounds like it could appear in any LinkedIn post about AI, rewrite it.
Keep the total output tight. Depth over breadth. A sharp 3-lens session beats a bloated 7-lens run.
