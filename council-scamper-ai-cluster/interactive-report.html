4 Redesign · 2 Holds · 1 Stress Point. The thesis is correct — the identity gap framing holds. The implementation is more protective than the thesis requires. The architecture defers the hard question at several points where it should be asking it.

Agreed Actions
Key Improvements — Stated

These are the changes the council converged on. They are ordered by implementation readiness — the first three cost nothing and can happen immediately. Items 4–6 require design work. Item 7 is a future experiment.

1

Compress the Efficiency layer from two sessions to one

The two-week Efficiency block was designed to surface the "faster content" ceiling — but dedicating that much time to it risks validating the trap rather than exposing it. One session is enough to name the ceiling, show the trap, and move on.

What changes: Session 2 becomes the single Efficiency session. The reclaimed week shifts into the Judgment layer design, which all four advisors agree is the most under-resourced part of the architecture. No structural redesign required — this is a scheduling change with an immediate effect on where attention lands.

Implement now
2

Add a pre-work Override Log before the Opening Expo

Participants currently arrive at the Opening Expo as an audience. Giving them seven days of unframed Override Log practice before they walk in converts them into analysts — they watch the practitioner presentations against a self-established baseline, not as aspirational spectators.

What changes: One prompt sent one week before the Opening Expo: "Note what you change when you use AI this week — what you accept, what you override, why." No framing, no rubric, no right answer. Seven days of low-stakes data collection. The Expo landing is fundamentally different when participants are comparing, not just watching.

Implement now
3

Reverse the Opening Expo narrative arc — lead with struggle, not arrival

The current Expo format presumably opens with practitioners demonstrating new capabilities — the destination. This creates aspiration but also distance. Starting instead with the moment before the thing clicked — what the practitioner couldn't do, where they got stuck, what they had to give up — changes how the ceiling lands.

What changes: Briefing practitioners to structure their presentations as a transition arc, not a capability showcase. The question shifts from "look what I can do" to "here's the work of getting here." Costs nothing. Requires only a revised brief to Expo presenters.

Implement now
4

Rename the three peer deep dive layers

"Efficiency / Judgment / Capability" are accurate descriptors but carry the wrong connotations. "Efficiency" is a management word that validates what participants came to move past. The vocabulary sets the frame before the session starts.

What changes: Rename to Surface Work (where AI saves time — and the trap), Standard Work (where you override AI — what taste and judgment are yours), and Original Work (what you can now do that wasn't possible before). The session content stays the same; the framing does not. "Surface Work" signals a ceiling to move through. "Efficiency" signals something to protect.

Implement now
5

Redesign the Judgment (Standard Work) layer as structured critique

This is the highest-leverage change in the architecture. Judgment is the hinge — it's where the identity work begins, and it's what must deliver participants ready for the Original Work frame. Currently the Judgment sessions are peer discussions. Peer discussion surfaces what people are comfortable saying. Structured critique surfaces the gap between intent and execution. That gap is where identity work actually starts.

What changes: Each Judgment session is redesigned around a formal critique structure borrowed from design education: participant shares an Override Log entry and states their intent; peers apply "rigor of reflection" as an explicit standard — not "what did you do" but "why did you override, and what standard are you holding that AI doesn't?" The participant responds. This isn't therapy and it isn't feedback. It's craft critique applied to a new kind of work.

Design required
6

Eliminate the Week 9 identity statement as a standalone deliverable

Week 9 currently asks participants to synthesize their Override Log patterns into a written identity statement before the Closing Expo. The problem: it creates a deliverable that participants optimise for the product rather than the discovery. A polished statement that smooths over rough edges is the opposite of what the Closing Expo is trying to surface.

What changes: Remove the standalone Week 9 statement. Instead, participants bring their three sharpest Override Log entries to the Closing Expo — the moments where their standard, taste, or judgment diverged most clearly from what AI suggested. The Expo preparation IS the synthesis. The Closing Expo presents those entries, not a distillation of them. The identity is demonstrated, not declared.

Design required
7

Activate the Closing Expo → Opening Expo alumni pipeline

The Closing Expo currently closes a cohort. It could also open the next one. Participants from one cohort's Closing Expo become the practitioners who set the ceiling at the next cohort's Opening Expo — replacing external practitioners with people who have lived the same transition arc the new cohort is about to enter.

What changes: After Cohort 1 closes, three to four participants are invited back for Cohort 2's Opening Expo. They present using the reversed narrative arc (improvement #3): where they were in Week 1, what shifted in the Judgment sessions, what they can claim as theirs now. The standard-raising is structural and compounding — each cohort's ceiling is set by the previous cohort's work, not by external practitioners whose context may not match.

Unresolved Friction
Key Tensions from the Council

Protection vs. Disruption — ran through every lens

The Transformer interpreted every design friction as a safety gap. The Pioneer interpreted every safety mechanism as a design compromise. The Architect's reframe was the most useful intervention: safety-vs-delay is not the same as safety-vs-no-safety. You can build a container that holds difficulty without removing the difficulty. But whether the architecture actually creates productive discomfort — or just defers it — was not resolved by the council.

Parties: Cautious Transformer vs. Untethered Pioneer, across all seven lenses

Unresolved

Form vs. Rawness — Closing Expo

Pioneer: the presentation format protects people from saying the actual thing. The Override Log should be read aloud, unmediated — strip the abstraction. Guardian: form is not abstraction. Unmediated exposure in a public context produces self-protection, not revelation. The sonnet isn't less honest than raw feeling; it's what makes feeling hold still long enough to mean something. You can't shortcut form and call it honesty.

Pioneer conceded in Round 2 that "curated, staged, read aloud" is itself a form — and that their argument was right but their execution proposal was wrong.

Parties: Pioneer vs. Craft Guardian · Pioneer partially conceded

Partially Resolved

Structural Iteration vs. Disruption Assumption

The Architect's solutions across all seven lenses were consistently the tighter version of the existing mechanism. Pre-work Override Log. Sharper Judgment sessions. Better Expo framing. The Pioneer's challenge in Round 2: the architecture has an assumption baked in that we never named — that participants will engage with identity transition if the container is well-designed. The Pioneer doesn't believe that. Identity transition requires a disruption the architecture keeps smoothing over at the last moment.

Parties: Pioneer vs. Architect · Pioneer argues that sophisticated iteration is not the same as redesign

Unresolved

Week 9 Identity Statement — Keep or Cut

Pioneer's argument: a deliverable that makes people optimise for product over discovery. When the hard thing has a deadline and a format, people write toward the format. The Transformer originally held that the statement was necessary synthesis — a way to consolidate before the Expo. In Round 2, the Transformer changed position: the Closing Expo IS the identity statement, demonstrated rather than declared. Cutting the standalone Week 9 deliverable frees the arc to land in the Expo rather than in a document the week before it.

Parties: Pioneer + Architect vs. Transformer · Transformer changed position in Round 2

Resolved — Eliminate (3 of 4 votes)
