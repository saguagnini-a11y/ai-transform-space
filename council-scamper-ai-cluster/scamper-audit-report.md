### Marit — The Constrained Corporate

*Senior L&D Advisor, regulated multinational. Daily AI user for surface tasks. Zero workflow redesign.*

#### SCAMPER Analysis

**Component 1: Bi-Weekly Cycle**

**Substitute — Show & Tell guest format**
The guest-led format puts a practitioner at the front demonstrating AI use. I'd substitute this with a compliance officer or governance lead co-presenting with the practitioner — not as gatekeeper but as explicit legitimiser. Right now "AI works" is the implicit message. I need "AI works AND can be defended to your audit committee." One without the other is unusable for me.

**Combine — Tiny Experiment + Override Log**
The Tiny Experiment currently floats free as a workplace action. If it were combined with the Override Log at the point of design — so participants write the three Override Log prompts *before* the experiment, as intention-setting, and again *after* as reflection — it creates documentation of responsible use that exists before anything is submitted for review. This isn't just pedagogically interesting; it's a paper trail.

**Modify — Sprint intensity and framing**
45 minutes of "hands-on experimentation" with "no theory" sounds like the kind of thing that would surface in a retrospective as "we just played around." I need that 45 minutes reframed. Not less hands-on, but more explicitly structured: what was the input, what were we testing, what was the output, what was the decision rule. Without that frame, the Sprint produces anecdote, not evidence.

**Eliminate — "No theory" framing**
The description says "no theory — hands-on experimentation." I understand the impulse but the framing is wrong for my context. Theory IS safety documentation. If I can't explain why I'm doing something, I can't defend it. I'd eliminate the "no theory" instruction and replace it with "no passive theory" — meaning we act, but we name what we're doing.

**Component 2: The Override Log**

**Substitute — Log format for structured decision record**
Three open questions is a diary. I need a structured decision record with consistent fields: date, decision type (aesthetic / ethical / accuracy / compliance), what AI suggested, what I overrode, rationale, outcome. This is a QA document, not a journal. The current framing won't survive review.

**Adapt — From learning reflection to professional evidence**
Legal and compliance fields use decision logs as standard practice. Audit trails in clinical trials document every departure from protocol with justification. The Override Log borrows from learning reflection but could adapt this professional evidence model — something that accumulates into a portfolio of defensible decisions, not just a record of interesting moments.

**Component 3: Deep Dive**

**Reverse — Start with the debrief format**
I know what "share what worked" sounds like in my organisation: a confessional, not an evidence review. I'd reverse the Deep Dive so that we define *at the start* what a good debrief looks like — what counts as evidence, what counts as inference, what counts as speculation. Then the Build phase becomes deliberate practice toward a defensible output, not exploration for its own sake.

**Component 4: Transformation Expo**

**Put to Another Use — Expo as stakeholder communication rehearsal**
80 experiments, 20 participants, 6 rotations. This could be repurposed as a stakeholder briefing exercise — not just sharing what you built, but practising the conversation you'd have with your director or compliance team. The insights captured across tables could feed a report template that participants take back to their organisations as a communication asset.

---

#### 3 Key Improvements

1. **Add a Governance Scaffolding Layer to the Override Log.** Not a redesign — an additional field set. Decision type (compliance / accuracy / ethical / aesthetic), context of override, downstream risk assessment. This converts a reflective tool into a professional artefact that survives organisational scrutiny. Participants in regulated contexts need to be able to submit this to a manager and have it read as rigorous, not experimental.

2. **Reframe the Sprint with an Explicit Methodology Card.** Before hands-on work begins, participants receive a one-page methodology card: hypothesis (what I'm testing), input (what I gave AI), output (what AI produced), decision (what I accepted / modified / rejected), rationale. This isn't about slowing down — it's about building the habit of documenting decisions at the moment they're made, not reconstructing them later. Without this, the Sprint produces stories, not evidence.

3. **The Transformation Expo needs a "Defensibility Audit" Rotation.** One of the six tables should be explicitly dedicated to: how would you defend this workflow redesign to your compliance team / line manager / audit committee? Not just "what did you build" but "what would you say if challenged." This reframes the Expo from a show-and-tell into a preparation for re-entry into regulated environments.

---

#### 3 Risks

1. **The governance anxiety mechanism: continuous filtering, not episodic resistance.** My threat radar isn't a switch I turn on when something looks dangerous — it's running constantly. Every design choice is filtered through "can I defend this in a meeting?" The risk is that the program's energy and pace feel incompatible with that ongoing cognitive overhead. If I'm managing anxiety the whole time while others are in flow, I'll attribute the discomfort to the program and conclude it "isn't for me" — without ever articulating that what I needed was permission to slow down.

2. **The comfort-threat mechanism: experimentation with no institutional frame.** My comfort is predictability, not laziness. I've spent years building a reputation on rigorous manual processes. The program explicitly asks me to experiment — which in my context means "doing things that haven't been approved." The risk is that I engage superficially (complete the minimum requirements, submit careful work) while never actually changing my behaviour outside sessions. Compliance without adoption.

3. **The identity threat mechanism: "reckless" as the worst professional label.** The program's framing is positive — "responsible innovation," "deliberate AI collaboration." But the framing I carry into the room is: one visible mistake could undo years of reputation. The risk is that any moment in the program where I'm asked to share unfinished work, show a "messy middle," or demonstrate an experiment that failed becomes a threat to my professional identity. I'll protect myself by polishing everything before sharing — and lose the learning the program is designed to produce.

#### Pre-Mortem

*It's 10 weeks from now. I dropped out. Here's why.*

I made it to week five. The first two cycles were fine — Show & Tell guests were credible, I had something to bring to the Sprint, and the Tiny Experiments I designed were genuinely useful. I even started a version of the Override Log that I thought could work. But around week five, during a Deep Dive, the facilitator asked us to share our "messy middle" — the moment where the AI output surprised us or where we didn't know what to do. And I froze.

Not because I didn't have an example. I had plenty. But everything I could have shared would have revealed that I was testing AI on content that hadn't been formally approved for AI-assisted production. The organisation hadn't explicitly prohibited it, but it hadn't approved it either. And I realised that if I shared the example, I'd be putting my work on record — in a shared space, with peers I don't fully know, in a program that encouraged openness. That was the moment I understood that "psychological safety" and "institutional safety" are not the same thing.

I stopped attending after week six. I told myself I was too busy. That wasn't entirely wrong. But what I didn't say — and didn't have the vocabulary to say — was that the program had no mechanism for the kind of risk I carry. It offered a space to experiment. It didn't offer a defensible institutional frame for the experiments. Those two things aren't the same. I needed the second one before I could use the first.

**Commitment Vote: CONDITIONAL**
Condition: The program must include explicit governance scaffolding — a documented framework for how Override Log entries and Tiny Experiment outputs could be presented in a stakeholder meeting. Not a workaround; a design feature.

### Yael — The Authentic Craft Professional

*Independent learning designer, boutique consultancy founder, 12+ years. Extensive AI use, hand-finishes everything.*

#### SCAMPER Analysis

**Component 1: Bi-Weekly Cycle**

**Substitute — Sprint format for creative critique session**
The Sprint is currently: design and test one micro-case using AI. That's an execution exercise. I'd substitute (or at minimum, add) a critique layer — after the AI output is produced, participants spend 10 minutes on structured peer critique: what's the quality difference between this output and the best version of this work you've seen? What did the AI do well? What did it miss? Without a critique layer, we're training fluency, not judgment. And judgment is the thing I've spent 12 years developing.

**Combine — Override Log + Reflection Pods into a craft dialogue session**
The Override Log asks three questions retrospectively. The Reflection Pods meet between sessions. These two should be folded together and repositioned as a live creative dialogue — two people showing each other their Override Log mid-sprint, not just after. The moment of override is when craft is happening. The current structure captures it after the fact and loses the texture.

**Adapt — From design studio crits to Override Log format**
Architecture and graphic design programs have crit culture — you bring work in progress, a peer interrogates your choices, you have to defend them out loud. The Override Log as currently framed is a private journal. If it adapted the studio crit model, it would become a semi-public artifact where the *quality of your override reasoning* is visible, not just the fact that you overrode. That's what distinguishes craft from preference.

**Modify — Deep Dive debrief: amplify the "what you overrode" section**
The current debrief is three things: what worked, what you overrode, what you'd take back to your team. "What you overrode" is one-third of the debrief. I'd amplify it significantly — make it the centrepiece. Ask: what principle was behind the override? Was it aesthetic, ethical, conceptual? What would you have lost if you hadn't overridden? This is where craft becomes visible and discussable, rather than a footnote to the "what worked" section.

**Component 2: The Override Log**

**Reverse — Retrospective log to live annotation**
Three prompts reviewed after the work is done misses the actual moment of decision. The best creative overrides happen in the middle of working, not when you're writing up what happened. I want a live annotation tool — a sidebar, a voice memo, a sticky note — where I capture the thought *as it happens*: "AI went flat here, I'm going warmer," "the structure is right but the register is wrong, taking it." Retrospective framing assumes memory is good enough to reconstruct creative decisions. It isn't.

**Put to Another Use — Override Log as client communication**
I currently struggle to articulate to clients what "AI-augmented" means for my work quality. The Override Log — if made into a curated artefact — could become a transparency document I share with clients: here's what AI contributed, here's where I directed it, here's where I made the critical judgment calls. This reframes AI-augmentation not as a cost-cutting signal but as evidence of deeper craft engagement.

**Component 3: Deep Dive**

**Substitute — "Greatest workflow challenge" for "sharpest creative problem"**
"Workflow challenge" is a management consulting frame. It invites people to bring process problems. I want to bring a *creative* problem — where the AI and I disagree about quality. The reframe matters because it signals what kind of work is welcome and positions the Deep Dive as a craft space rather than an efficiency clinic.

**Component 4: Transformation Expo**

**Eliminate — "80 experiments, 20 participants" framing**
Scale as a metric is the wrong signal for this audience. When the Expo is framed as "80 experiments," it foregrounds volume. I hear: AI made us faster and more productive. That's not what I want to take home. I want to hear: here's a workflow that changed what I thought was possible creatively. The Expo should be reframed around quality breakthroughs, not quantity metrics.

---

#### 3 Key Improvements

1. **The Override Log must be redesigned as a live capture tool, not a retrospective form.** Voice memo integration, margin annotation, or a lightweight mobile-first input that allows capture in the moment of override. The three questions remain, but they should be answerable in 30 seconds mid-sprint, not in 10 minutes of post-session reflection. Retrospective capture is reconstruction, not documentation. Craft decisions are lost in the gap.

2. **Add a structured craft critique layer to the Sprint.** After each micro-case is tested, 10 minutes of structured peer critique: what's the quality signal in this output? What did the AI miss that you had to correct? What does that correction tell you about your judgment? This isn't slowing down the Sprint — it's building the vocabulary for the Override Log and making quality visible as a collective practice, not just an individual preference.

3. **Separate the Override Log into two layers: private process and public artefact.** The live annotation layer is private — messy, in-progress, unpolished. But there should be a curatorial step where participants select 1-2 entries to make public within the cohort or, optionally, to share as a client-facing transparency document. This respects the intimacy of creative process while creating a mechanism for craft to become visible and discussable at the group level.

---

#### 3 Risks

1. **The deflation mechanism: leading with speed/scale triggers not-being-seen.** Every time the program foregrounds efficiency ("80 experiments," "minimum viable," "ship it"), I experience it as a signal that what's being valued is throughput, not quality. The risk is that I attend dutifully but disengage emotionally — show up, complete the tasks, never bring the work that actually matters to me because I don't believe this is a space that can see it. I'll be physically present but creatively absent.

2. **The identity threat mechanism: AI as the source of capability.** If the program consistently frames AI as "what makes this possible," it positions AI as the agent and participants as beneficiaries. My professional identity is built on being the source of capability — I've been doing things that others hadn't done before. The risk is that the framing of the program slowly erodes the language I use to talk about my own work. I'll start describing my process in AI terms rather than craft terms. That's not a skill gain; it's an identity loss.

3. **The community mismatch mechanism: cohort at the wrong level.** If the cohort is primarily early-stage AI users (Marit types — surface tasks only), every session becomes implicit remediation for people who haven't started yet. I'll end up spending my Override Log entries explaining why I overrode something that most participants wouldn't have questioned at all. The craft conversation I'm looking for requires peers who are already past the "AI is interesting" stage. Without them, I'm the most advanced person in the room, and that's lonely in a different way from Daan's loneliness.

#### Pre-Mortem

*It's 10 weeks from now. I dropped out. Here's why.*

I lasted seven weeks. The first three were promising — Show & Tell had a guest who was doing something I hadn't seen before with research synthesis, and the Override Log format gave me a way to think about something I'd been doing intuitively without naming it. I thought: this might actually be a place where the craft conversation I've been looking for exists.

Then week four happened. We were in the Sprint and the facilitator said, almost as encouragement, "look how fast we can produce this." And the room lit up. People were proud of the speed. They were comparing outputs — "mine took 8 minutes," "I did three iterations in 12 minutes." And I realised I'd been doing something completely different with the same tool. I'd been going slower on purpose. Pausing. Interrogating the AI's word choices. Rewriting the structure it gave me because it was technically correct and creatively inert. I wasn't doing it faster. I was doing it differently.

I said something in the debrief about that. About how speed wasn't the signal I was optimising for. The response was polite but I could tell it landed as an edge case — "Yael has different needs." Which is exactly the problem. I don't want to be the edge case with different needs. I want to be in a room where craft judgment is the default frame, not an alternative perspective. I stopped coming after week seven. I told my contact that "the timing didn't work." She probably believed me. It was easier than explaining that I needed a room full of Daans, not a room full of Marits.

Condition: The Override Log must support live capture (not just retrospective), and the cohort composition must include at least a third of participants with prior workflow redesign experience.

### Daan — The Stranded Pioneer

*L&D Manager, mid-to-large org. Built chatbot assessments, automated pipelines on personal devices. 18 months ahead of peers. Works alone.*

#### SCAMPER Analysis

**Component 1: Bi-Weekly Cycle**

**Substitute — Participant entry point for differentiated tracks**
The bi-weekly cycle assumes a single starting level. Step 1 (Show & Tell) works if you're being shown something you haven't seen before. I've already built what most Show & Tell guests will probably demonstrate. What I need is a cycle that starts at a different level — not "here's what AI can do" but "here's a workflow problem at the organisational level, how do we solve it." If the program can't differentiate the starting point, it's designed for someone who hasn't started yet. I've been doing this for 18 months.

**Combine — Tiny Experiment + governance documentation**
My Tiny Experiments aren't tiny. They're patches on systems I've built outside sanctioned channels. The "minimum viable experiment" framing is useful for beginners; it underestimates what experienced practitioners are actually shipping. If the Tiny Experiment were combined with a governance documentation template — a brief that describes the experiment in terms a director could read — it would start doing the organisational bridging work I actually need. Right now it's just a commitment mechanism. I need it to double as a translation layer.

**Adapt — Pair programming model from software development**
In software, experienced developers pair with newer ones — the senior person navigates, the junior drives. The Deep Dive could adapt this: advanced participants pair with early-stage ones, not to teach but to work on the advanced participant's actual problem using the early-stage participant as a structured sounding board. The early-stage person gains exposure to a real-world workflow problem; the advanced person gains structured reflection on work they've been doing alone. The Override Log produced in these sessions would be richer for both.

**Reverse — Sprint sequence: start with the override**
The Sprint currently runs: design → test → produce. I'd reverse it: start by specifying in advance what you will NOT let the AI do, and why. Define your override criteria before you prompt. Then run the sprint. Then compare your pre-stated override criteria to what you actually overrode. This inverts the learning dynamic — instead of discovering what you overrode after the fact, you're testing your own assumptions about your judgment. That's a more sophisticated exercise and it's more honest about how AI-assisted work actually happens.

**Component 2: The Override Log**

**Put to Another Use — Override Log as governance document**
The Override Log is framed as a personal reflection tool. It should double as a governance artefact. Three additional fields: decision context (was this a quality decision, a compliance decision, or a strategic decision?), organisational implication (what would have happened if this decision had been made by AI alone?), documentation trail (is this override recorded anywhere in official systems?). With those fields, the Override Log becomes the document I bring to my director when I'm trying to explain why the chatbot assessment I built at home should be adopted by the organisation. Right now I have capability but no institutional vocabulary. The Override Log — properly framed — could be that vocabulary.

**Modify — Reflection Pods: extend scope, reduce frequency**
10 minutes between sessions is not enough for the conversations I want to have. Two peers, three prompts, 10 minutes — that's a check-in, not a dialogue. I'd reduce the frequency (every two sessions rather than every session) and extend the time to 30 minutes. Fewer but deeper. The questions would shift too: not just "what did you ship" but "what are you not shipping because it's not in sanctioned systems, and what would it take to change that?"

**Component 3: Deep Dive**

**Substitute — "Greatest workflow challenge" for "organisational adoption problem"**
The Deep Dive invitation says "pick your greatest workflow challenge." My workflow isn't the challenge. I've solved the workflow. My challenge is getting the organisation to recognise and adopt what I've built. The Deep Dive should offer a track for this — not "how do I build this" but "how do I get my organisation to trust and adopt this." That's a different skill set and it's the skill gap I actually have.

**Component 4: Transformation Expo**

**Combine — Expo with explicit "shadow AI work" visibility mechanism**
I have 18 months of shadow AI work that doesn't count. The Expo has 80 experiments — but I'd bet most of them are task-level improvements, not workflow redesigns. I'd add a specific track to the Expo for "work that exists outside official channels" — a structured way to surface and validate what practitioners have built unofficially. This does two things: it gives people like me a legitimate venue for work that's been invisible, and it reveals to the program designers just how much AI adoption has already happened outside their frame.

---

#### 3 Key Improvements

1. **Create an Advanced Track with differentiated entry point and session content.** Not a separate program — a parallel track within the same sessions. Advanced participants start with the assumption of workflow redesign experience and their Show & Tell, Sprint, and Deep Dive content is pitched at organisational integration and adoption problems, not AI tool use. This requires only a pre-session diagnostic (3 questions about current AI practice) and a clear communication of which track is which. Without this, the program will lose its most experienced participants in weeks two or three.

2. **Redesign the Override Log to include a governance layer, with an explicit "institutional translation" field.** One additional field: "What would I need to write for my director to trust this decision?" This doesn't replace the reflective questions — it adds a translation step. The Override Log becomes both a personal record and a stakeholder communication tool. For me, this is the difference between the program being useful and the program being interesting. Interesting doesn't get me the recognition I need.

3. **The Reflection Pods need a "shadow work" prompt.** Current prompts: what did you ship, show the artifact, what can we do now that we couldn't before. Add: what are you working on that you can't show officially? What would it take to make it visible? This opens the conversation I need to have — not about what we're building in the program, but about the gap between what we're building privately and what our organisations can see.

---

#### 3 Risks

1. **The entry-level design mechanism: designed for someone who hasn't started yet.** The risk is that the program's default register assumes participants need to be convinced that AI is worth using. Every Show & Tell that demonstrates a basic AI use case, every Sprint that starts from scratch, every Tiny Experiment that frames itself as "minimum viable" signals that the program is for the beginning of the journey. I'm 18 months in. The dissonance between where I am and where the program assumes I am will accumulate. By week three I'll feel like I'm sitting in a masterclass I already know.

2. **The cognitive dependency anxiety mechanism: meta-awareness of the risk.** I know I'm addicted to building with AI ("een soort verslaving"). The program is asking me to do more of exactly what I'm already concerned about. The risk isn't that I'll disengage — it's that I'll over-engage, build more shadow work during the program, and emerge 10 weeks later with more unofficial capability and no better path to recognition. The program could accelerate the exact problem it's designed to help me solve.

3. **The isolation mechanism: nobody at the same level.** I came hoping for peers. If the cohort is primarily Marits and Yaels, the conversation will be at the level they need — which is not the level I need. I'll end up in an implicit teaching role I didn't sign up for. That's exhausting in a specific way: you perform generosity while experiencing loneliness. I've been doing this alone for 18 months. I don't need to do it alone in a group setting.

#### Pre-Mortem

*It's 10 weeks from now. I dropped out. Here's why.*

I stayed for six weeks, which is longer than I expected. The first session was actually good — there was a Show & Tell guest who was doing something with automated pipeline design that I hadn't seen framed that way before, and I thought: maybe there are people here who are further along than I assumed. That first session bought me three more.

That was the moment I understood: this program is optimised for participants who haven't started yet, and it has no infrastructure for practitioners who have. The Tiny Experiment asks me to commit to "one small workplace experiment." I'm running 12 ongoing experiments and none of them are small. The Override Log asks me to reflect on one override. My whole practice is an override. I left after week six. Not angry. Just disappointed in a specific way — the loneliness I'd been carrying around didn't get lighter. It got heavier, because I'd allowed myself to think this time it might be different.

### Alliance Map

**Strong alignments:**
- Yael + Daan on Override Log redesign: both want live/real-time capture; both reject purely retrospective format. Yael wants it for craft annotation; Daan wants it for governance documentation. Different motivations, compatible direction.
- Marit + Daan on governance layer in Override Log: both want it to serve institutional purposes. Marit for defensibility; Daan for recognition. Neither is satisfied with a reflection journal.
- Daan → Marit on Advanced Track: Daan's request for differentiated entry resonates with Marit's implicit wish not to be the "cautious" participant slowing down the room. She supports it partly because it might take the most experimental participants somewhere else.

**Active tensions:**
- Marit vs Yael on Override Log privacy: Marit wants a structured, auditable record (maximally structured, minimally personal); Yael wants a live, intimate creative capture tool (minimally structured, maximally personal). These are directionally opposite.
- Yael vs Daan on cohort composition: Yael wants craft-focused peers at high AI maturity; Daan wants workflow-redesign peers at organisational level. They may both want a room without Marit, but they're not describing the same room.
- Daan vs Marit on pace and risk tolerance: Daan's "shadow AI work" visibility request alarms Marit. She experiences his approach to unsanctioned building as precisely the kind of "recklessness" she's trying not to be associated with.

**Unresolvable tension:**
Yael's Override Log must be live, intimate, and unpolished. Marit's Override Log must be structured, documentable, and defensible. Both are legitimate. They cannot both be served by the same tool without one of them compromising the core of what they need. Either the Override Log is a private creative diary that can be selectively surfaced (Yael's version), or it's a professional documentation system with standardised fields (Marit's version). These aren't two ends of a spectrum; they're different tools for different jobs.

---

### The Debate

**On the Override Log**

**Daan to Marit:** "You want the Override Log to be a compliance document. I get it — you need to be able to show it to your director and have it read as rigorous. But the moment you standardise the fields and make it auditable, you kill the thing that makes it valuable. The point of the Override Log is that it captures the moment where your judgment diverged from AI's. That moment isn't always clean. Sometimes I can't explain in two sentences why I overrode something. I just knew it was wrong. If the log requires me to explain in structured fields every time, I'll stop using it honestly and start using it performatively."

**Marit to Daan:** "That's fine for you. You're working outside sanctioned channels anyway — what's one more undocumented decision? I'm not. I'm in a regulated environment where 'I just knew it was wrong' is not a defensible position. If I put something in a log that reads like a diary, and that log gets reviewed in an audit — and it will, if AI-assisted work becomes standard — then I haven't protected myself. I've created a liability. You need the Override Log to prove craft. I need it to prove competence. Those are different standards."

**Yael to both:** "You're both treating the Override Log as if its primary purpose is institutional. Mine is entirely different. I want to capture the moment a creative decision happens because those moments are how I understand what I actually believe about quality. If you turn it into a compliance document, you'll get compliance. You won't get thinking. And the one thing that has any chance of keeping my work distinct from commodity AI output is that I'm doing actual thinking about why I'm making the choices I'm making. That thinking has to live somewhere."

**Daan to Yael:** "I agree with you more than it sounds like. But here's the problem: your version of the Override Log is for you alone. Marit's version — even though I find it stifling — is the one that gets AI-assisted work recognised at the organisational level. If we only build the craft version, the institutional case never gets made. The work stays invisible. I've been doing beautiful, thoughtful, deliberate work for 18 months and it counts for nothing because there's no document that a director can read. I need Marit's version to exist even if I don't use it the way she does."

**Marit to Yael:** "I actually want what you're describing. I want to do thinking-level work, not just surface tasks. But I can't get there from where I am without something institutional first. The Override Log as a governance document isn't the destination — it's the permission slip. If I can show my organisation that AI-assisted work can be rigorous and documented, then I can start doing the kind of creative AI work you're describing. You're already past the permission step. ## Key Tensions

1. **Override Log architecture is genuinely bifurcated.** Yael needs live, intimate, minimally structured. Marit needs auditable, standardised, institutional. These can coexist in a two-layer design (private process / public document) but the program must actively maintain both without collapsing into whichever one the majority of a given cohort finds natural. Risk: facilitators drift toward whichever version produces the most visible artefacts.

2. **Differentiated entry vs cohort cohesion.** Daan needs an advanced track; Marit might benefit from one (removes the pressure of keeping pace with pioneers); Yael needs a cohort of advanced peers. But splitting the cohort by experience level risks eliminating the cross-pollination that makes the Transformation Expo valuable. 80 experiments from a mixed cohort are more interesting than 80 experiments from a homogeneous one.

3. **Speed as signal, quality as undercurrent.** The program's energy is oriented toward shipping — "hands-on experimentation," "minimum viable experiment," "what did you ship." This is motivating for early-stage participants and alienating for practitioners whose value proposition is quality, not throughput. The framing cannot be neutral on this; it signals what the program values.

4. **Institutional recognition vs individual learning.** Daan and Marit both need the program to produce organisationally legible outcomes. Yael needs it to produce personally legible craft development. The program is currently designed to produce individual learning outcomes. Whether it can also produce institutional recognition mechanisms is an open design question.

---

## Consensus Points

1. **The Override Log is the most valuable component.** All three personas engage with it most deeply and all three have substantive ideas for improving it. It's also the component with the most design risk — it needs to serve too many purposes.

2. **The Tiny Experiment needs a documentation layer.** All three personas (in different ways) want the Tiny Experiment to produce something more durable than a commitment — a record, a governance brief, a governance artefact. The current framing as "minimum viable experiment" undersells what the component could do.

3. **The Deep Dive's "greatest workflow challenge" framing is too broad.** All three personas want to rename or reframe it for their context. This suggests the invitation language needs to offer multiple frames: workflow challenge / creative problem / adoption problem.

4. **The Transformation Expo is the component with the most untapped potential.** All three personas see different repurposing opportunities — stakeholder communication rehearsal (Marit), quality breakthrough showcase (Yael), shadow work visibility mechanism (Daan). The current framing as "scale showcase" (80 experiments) is the least interesting version of what it could be.

---

## Decision Framework

The program as currently designed will work well for participants in the early-to-middle range of AI adoption — practitioners who are using AI for surface tasks and want to go deeper. It will lose participants at both ends: Marits who need institutional legitimacy before they can engage, and Daans who are already beyond the program's assumed starting point.

**Three design decisions that determine the program's range:**

1. **Does the Override Log support live capture?** If yes: expands accessibility for advanced practitioners and craft-focused participants. If no: the program signals that reflection is the mode, not integration.

2. **Is there a differentiated entry track?** If yes: the program can serve a wider experience range without alienating advanced participants. If no: the program optimises for the median and loses the extremes.

3. **Does the program produce institutionally legible outputs?** If yes: the program can operate as both a learning experience and a professional development portfolio. If no: the program remains a personal learning initiative, which is valuable but insufficient for Marit and Daan.

**Recommended decision:** Implement the two-layer Override Log (private process / public document), build a three-question entry diagnostic that routes participants to differentiated tracks, and redesign the Transformation Expo to include an explicit "organisational adoption" conversation alongside the standard workflow showcase. These three changes extend the program's range without requiring a fundamental redesign. They are also the three changes that all three personas can agree on, even if they can't agree on much else.
