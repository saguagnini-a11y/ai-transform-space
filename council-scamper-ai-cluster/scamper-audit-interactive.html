The Debate — Fight Highlights

Three sharpest exchanges. Each persona defends from their behavioral position. At least one tension has no resolution.

Exchange 1 — Override Log: compliance document vs craft tool vs governance artefact
D
Daan → Marit
You want the Override Log to be a compliance document. I get it. But the moment you standardise the fields and make it auditable, you kill the thing that makes it valuable. The point is that it captures the moment where your judgment diverged from AI's. Sometimes I can't explain in two sentences why I overrode something. I just knew it was wrong. If the log requires structured fields every time, I'll stop using it honestly and start using it performatively.
M
Marit → Daan
"I just knew it was wrong" is fine for you — you're working outside sanctioned channels anyway. I'm not. I'm in a regulated environment where that is not a defensible position. If I put something in a log that reads like a diary and it gets reviewed in an audit — and it will — I haven't protected myself. I've created a liability. You need the Override Log to prove craft. I need it to prove competence. Those are different standards.
Y
Yael → Both
You're both treating the Override Log as if its primary purpose is institutional. Mine is entirely different. I want to capture the moment a creative decision happens because those moments are how I understand what I actually believe about quality. If you turn it into a compliance document, you'll get compliance. You won't get thinking.
Exchange 2 — The institutional visibility argument (Daan shifts Yael's vote)
D
Daan → Yael
Your version of the Override Log is for you alone. Marit's version — even though I find it stifling — is the one that gets AI-assisted work recognised at the organisational level. If we only build the craft version, the institutional case never gets made. The work stays invisible. I've been doing deliberate, thoughtful work for 18 months and it counts for nothing because there's no document a director can read. I need Marit's version to exist even if I don't use it the way she does.

That reframe landed. Not because I've changed my mind about what I need — but because I'd been reading the governance argument as a threat to craft. Daan reframed it as a precondition for visibility. Y
Yael → Marit
So you need a different version of the Override Log to get to the point where you can use the version I need. I understand that. But I'm not willing to wait while the program optimises for the permission-slip version. They need to be separate tools. Not a spectrum. Separate.
M
Marit → Yael

Unresolvable Tension

Yael's Override Log must be live, intimate, unpolished. Marit's must be structured, documentable, defensible. These can coexist in a two-layer design but the program must actively maintain both. Facilitators will drift toward whichever version produces the most visible artefacts. This is a facilitation and culture problem, not a design problem — and it has no clean solution.

Synthesis

Key Tensions & Consensus Points

Tensions — No Clean Resolution

Override Log is structurally bifurcated

Yael needs live, intimate, minimally structured. Marit needs auditable, standardised, institutional. A two-layer design can coexist but the facilitation culture will collapse it toward one or the other over time.

Differentiated entry vs cohort cohesion

An Advanced Track serves Daan and protects Yael. But splitting the cohort by experience risks eliminating the cross-pollination that makes the Transformation Expo valuable. 80 experiments from a mixed cohort are richer than 80 from a homogeneous one.

Speed as signal, quality as undercurrent

"Minimum viable experiment," "what did you ship" — the program's energy orientation signals throughput. Motivating for early-stage participants; alienating for practitioners whose value proposition is quality. The framing cannot be neutral.

Institutional recognition vs individual learning

Daan and Marit both need the program to produce organisationally legible outcomes. Yael needs personally legible craft development. The program is currently designed for individual learning. Whether it can also produce institutional recognition mechanisms is an open question.

Consensus — All Three Agree

The Override Log is the most valuable component

All three personas engage with it most deeply and all three have substantive ideas for improving it. It's also the component with the most design risk — it needs to serve too many purposes.

Tiny Experiment needs a documentation layer

All three want the Tiny Experiment to produce something more durable than a commitment — a record, a governance brief, an institutional artefact. "Minimum viable" undersells what the component could do.

Deep Dive framing is too generic

"Greatest workflow challenge" is too broad. All three want to reframe it for their context: workflow challenge / creative problem / adoption problem. The invitation language needs to offer multiple frames.

Transformation Expo has the most untapped potential

All three see different repurposing opportunities. The current "scale showcase" (80 experiments) is the least interesting version. Stakeholder communication rehearsal, quality breakthrough showcase, and shadow work visibility all fit within the existing structure.

Decision Framework

Three Design Decisions That Determine the Program's Range

The program works well for the early-to-middle range of AI adoption. It loses participants at both ends. These three decisions determine how far the range extends.

1
Does the Override Log support live capture?
If yes: expands accessibility for advanced practitioners and craft-focused participants. Signals that integration is the mode, not retrospective reflection. Requires a lightweight mobile-first capture interface or voice memo integration.
YES → Serves Yael + Daan, doesn't hurt Marit NO → Program signals reflection over integration; Yael and Daan both disengage
2
Is there a differentiated entry track?
If yes: the program can serve a wider experience range without alienating advanced participants. Requires a three-question pre-session diagnostic and clear communication of which track is which — not a separate program, a parallel track within the same sessions.
YES → Daan stays, Yael gets peers at her level, Marit benefits from pressure release NO → Program optimises for the median; loses both ends of the experience spectrum by week four
3
Does the program produce institutionally legible outputs?
If yes: the program operates as both a learning experience and a professional development portfolio. The Override Log (with governance layer), the Tiny Experiment (with documentation brief), and the Transformation Expo (with defensibility rotation) all produce artefacts that participants can bring back to their organisations.
YES → Marit and Daan can justify continued participation; Yael's craft also becomes legible to clients NO → Program remains a personal learning initiative; valuable but insufficient for Marit and Daan

Recommended Decision

Implement the two-layer Override Log (private process / public document), build a three-question entry diagnostic that routes participants to differentiated tracks, and redesign the Transformation Expo to include an explicit "organisational adoption" conversation alongside the standard workflow showcase.

These three changes extend the program's range without requiring fundamental redesign. They are the changes that all three personas can agree on — even if they can't agree on much else.
