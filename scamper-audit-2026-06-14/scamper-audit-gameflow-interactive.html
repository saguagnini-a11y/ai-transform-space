The Debate

Where the personas couldn't agree

Four exchanges where the disagreement is structural — not just preference. At least one has no clean resolution.

Exchange 1 — Is the verdict an instrument or a decoration?
DAAN
The verdict is self-certifying. I rated my problem 🟢 in the scan, picked a gold category, and got a 🟢 verdict. The two steps confirmed each other. That's not a diagnostic — it's a mirror.
MARIT
The mirror is the point. The game isn't testing whether your self-assessment is correct. It's giving you a structured way to articulate a self-assessment you can then defend. That's exactly what I need before I walk into a room with a compliance officer.
DAAN
So the game is a formatting tool, not a diagnostic tool.
MARIT
All diagnosis begins with self-report. You're expecting the game to know more about your organisation than you do.
YAEL
Marit's right that it starts with self-report. But then the game presents the output as a verdict, which implies external validation. It should present it as a draft — "here's your self-assessment, now go test it."
Exchange 2 — Should 🔴 problems have a pathway, not just a redirect?
DAAN
Two of my three problems rated 🔴. The game correctly said they weren't AI problems. Then it had nothing else to say. "Redirect — start here before AI" is not a pathway. It's a door with no handle.
MARIT
The 🔴 verdict IS the pathway. It tells you to fix the underlying problem before adding AI. That stops you building the wrong thing.
DAAN
It stops me, full stop. The game gives me a verdict and then sends me to the World Map. My 🔴 problems are still my most important problems. What do I do with them?
YAEL
If the game tries to address institutional blockages, it becomes a change management programme. That's not what it is.
DAAN
Then say so. "This is a problem for a different programme" is more honest than "Redirect — start here before AI."
MARIT
I agree with that. Name what the game is and isn't for.
Exchange 3 — Does the quick-scan find the right problem or the most tractable one? (No clean resolution)
YAEL
The scan moved me from challenge 1 to challenge 2. But challenge 2 was 🟢 because I've never built a reuse system — not because building one is necessarily right. The absence of a system isn't always a problem. Sometimes it's a choice. The scan treats all structural absences the same.
DAAN
My most important challenge is a governance blockage. The scan called it 🔴 and moved me away from it. The scan optimises for tractability, not importance.
MARIT
Are those different things? If a problem isn't tractable, is it one you should be working on right now?
YAEL
Yes. The intractable problems are often the ones that matter most. The scan creates a selection bias toward solvable problems.
MARIT
I'd rather have a solvable problem I can make progress on than an important problem I can't touch. That's prioritisation, not avoidance.
DAAN
That works if you're working within an institution. If you're trying to change the institution, you have to work on the intractable problems.
⚡ This exchange has no resolution. Both positions are correct for different contexts. The game cannot serve both simultaneously without explicit scoping.
Exchange 4 — Is the fluency of the output a feature or a bug?
YAEL
The game produces well-structured statements from thin inputs. Someone can type two-word answers and get a grammatically clean POV statement that sounds like a design brief. It isn't one.
DAAN
A fluent-sounding problem statement gets into rooms that a messy one doesn't. I'd rather have a well-formatted wrong answer than a correct answer nobody can read.
YAEL
You're describing a communication tool, not a diagnostic tool.
DAAN
Correct. And communication is the thing most practitioners actually need help with. They understand their problems. They can't make them legible to the people who approve budgets.
MARIT
He's right. The game's output is more useful for what comes after than for what happens during. It's a brief-generator more than a diagnosis-engine.
YAEL
Then be honest about that. Don't call it a "verdict." Call it a "draft brief."

I didn't drop out formally. I became a spectator.

Drops out at: Week 2

The game was fine. Fast. My problem statement was accurate. I went into Session 1 thinking someone else would have problems as specific as mine — workflow architecture, system integration. Instead: 45 minutes on prompt engineering for a quiz generator.

The workflow integration I'd described in "one small thing" requires HR system access I don't have, an API integration needing IT approval, and a governance document I'd need to write. The Sprint gave me 45 minutes and a whiteboard.

Could have been prevented: A capability-level filter in the participant selection, or a parallel track for systems-level work.
Tensions & Consensus

What the debate surfaced

Unresolved tensions

Tension 1
Tractability vs Importance
The quick-scan optimises for finding the most AI-tractable problem. For practitioners whose most important problems are governance, culture, or institutional design, the game correctly tells them those aren't AI problems — and then has nothing else to offer. It cannot address this without becoming a change management programme. It should at minimum acknowledge the limitation explicitly.
Tension 2
Legibility vs Accuracy
The POV template produces fluent, meeting-ready output. Genuinely useful for communicating upward. May also produce confident-sounding misdiagnoses. The game has no quality gate on the POV — any input generates a valid-looking output. Making the statement more legible may reduce the pressure players feel to think carefully.
Tension 3
Consistency Check vs Self-Report
The quick-scan and World 3 category selection are designed to be independent checks. In practice, a player who rates their problem 🟢 in the scan will almost always pick a gold-tier category in World 3, confirming the rating rather than checking it. The triangulation warning partially addresses this but only fires on obvious keyword mismatches. Subtle mismatches — and belief-level blocks — pass through undetected.

Consensus points

Consensus 1
The quick-scan is the most valuable addition
All three personas found it redirected them correctly. Marit moved from compliance (🔴) to transfer capture (🟢). Yael moved from briefing quality (🟡) to reuse layer (🟢). Daan was correctly told his most important problems weren't AI problems — uncomfortable, but accurate. The scan is the one step that functions as a check rather than just a capture.
Consensus 2
The capacity reframe is working
All three personas wrote about what becomes possible, not what becomes faster. Marit wrote about a 30-day check-in system. Yael wrote about making her own decision-making legible. Daan wrote about a quality gate that can't exist today. The reframe did its job on all three.
Consensus 3
The "why" field is the game's weakest link
All three wanted more scaffolding. Quality of output depends heavily on quality of input. The triangulation system, POV step, and "one small thing" all inherit from what players write here. A two-word answer produces a two-word diagnosis wearing a fluent costume.
Consensus 4
The game is better understood as a brief-generator than a diagnostic engine
All three accepted, by the end of the debate, that the game's most valuable output is a communication artefact for what comes after — a brief that gets the meeting that enables the project. Not a final diagnosis. Calling the output a "verdict" overstates what the game can actually know.
Consensus 5
The 🔴 verdict needs a "what next" pathway
All three agreed "Redirect — start here before AI" is useful but incomplete. It names the problem type without giving any direction. Even one sentence pointing toward what kind of intervention would help would do significant work.
Decision Framework

What to prioritise and what can wait

Prioritise first — highest leverage, broadest agreement
1

Scaffold the "why" field. Add 2–3 sub-questions as ghost prompts: "When exactly does this break down? Who's affected when it does? What would have to be true for it not to happen?" This improves triangulation accuracy, POV quality, and "one small thing" relevance simultaneously. All three personas wanted it. Highest leverage single change.

2

Add "what next" copy to the 🔴 verdict. Even one sentence: "This isn't an AI problem yet. The change that needs to happen first is [problem type]. Come back when that's in motion." Universal agreement. Low implementation cost.

3

Split the 🔴 tier into motivation (won't) vs governance/structural (can't). Different problems, different remedies. The current conflation forces players to pick the nearest approximation, not the true cause. This also addresses Daan's institutional-blockage blind spot.

Can wait — valid but lower urgency
4

Amend "one small thing" to name the judgment alongside the possibility. Yael's suggestion. High craft value. Lower urgency than the structural fixes above.

5

Add solo practitioner note to category descriptions. Edge case addressed in the facilitator guide before the UI.

6

Rename "verdict" to "draft brief" or "AI fit assessment." Accurate framing. But naming is the last thing to fix — fix the substance first.
