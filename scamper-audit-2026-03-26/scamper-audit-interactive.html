3

Round 2 — Cross-Persona Debate

Fight Highlights

Exchange 1 — Language adaptation vs. language resistance (Marit vs. Yael)
Marit
You voted no on renaming the Tiny Experiment. I'm not asking for watered-down content — I'm asking for language that allows me to survive a manager asking what I did this afternoon. "Validated Application" is not a euphemism. It's the institutional register I work in. You work independently. You don't have to explain your experiments to anyone.
Yael
And that's exactly the problem I'm diagnosing. You're asking the program to adapt to institutional cultures that treat professional learning as a compliance risk. I'm asking the program to help you argue for better cultures. Those are incompatible purposes. If I design the language so you can survive your manager's question, I've accepted that your manager's question is reasonable. I haven't.
Marit
That's a luxury position. You have 12 years of reputation to spend. I have three months of political capital at a time. I can't argue for better organizational cultures while also trying to change anything concrete. I need a path through the constraint I'm actually in, not the one I wish I had.
Yael
Then the program should name that constraint and help you challenge it — not design around it. If the program just makes constraint more comfortable, it reproduces it. You said yourself you want to be a responsible innovator, not a compliant executor. You can't get there by describing your experiments as validated applications.
Marit
And you can't get there at all if you resign from your organization before you've changed anything.
No resolution — both positions are internally coherent and serve the person holding them.
Exchange 2 — What the Override Log is actually for (Daan vs. Yael)
Daan
The Override Log is the most valuable component in this program, and you want to turn it into a craft journal. I need it to be a governance document — evidence I can show a director. Your additional prompts — "what would a less experienced practitioner have done here?" — that's craft differentiation. My director doesn't care about craft differentiation. My director cares about whether the AI-assisted output is defensible.
Yael
And what I'm saying is that "defensible" is a lower bar than "excellent." If the Override Log only trains practitioners to defend their decisions, it produces competent explainers. I'm trying to produce practitioners who can name what's distinctly theirs. Those are not the same thing.
Daan
In my organization, if I can't defend it, it doesn't get used. The craft excellence is real — I care about it too — but excellence that never crosses from shadow work to sanctioned system doesn't change anything. Your portfolio document is beautiful. It lives on your laptop. My governance document goes to a director meeting and changes whether AI gets officially adopted.
Yael
And my practitioner portfolio goes to a client meeting and changes whether I get hired. We're both making institutional arguments — mine is just to a different institution. The program shouldn't pick one of us. It should design an Override Log with enough structural depth that both of us can use it for what we actually need.
No resolution — the Override Log cannot serve all three purposes simultaneously without structural differentiation.
Exchange 5 — What is this program actually for? (All three)
Yael
I want this program to help practitioners see AI as a collaborator they've chosen, not a tool they've adopted. The Override Log is the right frame — but only if the program centers the practitioner's judgment as the protagonist.
Daan
I want this program to give me the organizational standing to make the work I've already done count. The protagonist for me is the institutional pathway, not the individual decision.
Marit
I want this program to give me a legitimate path from where I am to somewhere further, inside constraints that are real. The protagonist for me is the responsible innovator I'm trying to become — but I need to become her on my organization's terms, not on the program's terms.
Yael
Those are three different programs.
Daan
Or three different tracks in one program.
Marit
Or one program that decides who it's actually for.
The sharpest unresolved tension in the audit — three coherent visions, one program.
Round 2

Mind-Change Log

Marit No vote change

Daan's Legitimacy Track framing moved her most. She hadn't named "shadow work" as a category that applied to her — she framed it as "things I've tested but can't yet deploy." Recognizing that she also has informal, unacknowledged work is a reframe with implications.

More worried now: the Override Log is being asked to do three incompatible things simultaneously. She'd assumed the fourth prompt wouldn't change what the log was for Yael or Daan. The debate made clear it can't do all three without becoming incoherent.

Yael No vote change

Daan's point — "excellence that never crosses from shadow work to sanctioned system doesn't change anything" — landed harder than expected. Yael had treated institutional translation as someone else's problem.

More worried now: the Override Log cannot be a craft portfolio AND a governance document AND a legitimization tool. The program needs to decide which it's optimizing for — or offer structurally different versions. Deeper design problem than she'd diagnosed in Round 1.

Daan Vote changed: NO → Conditional

Two things moved him. Marit's reframe of capability tracks as constraint environment tracks — same structure, different organizing principle. Tracks organized by what participants are navigating rather than how advanced they are makes the program more honest without creating hierarchy.

Yael's depth track alternative (task → process → practice) suggested a design axis organized around purpose rather than level, which might serve more people.

Still: neither suggestion gives his 18 months of shadow work a path to legitimization. The Legitimacy Track remains his most urgent need and neither persona engaged with it deeply enough.

Design Tensions

No Clean Resolution

Language adaptation vs. language resistance
Marit: adapt the program's language to survive institutional contexts. Yael: resist, because adapting language to bureaucratic mediocrity reproduces it. The program cannot simultaneously signal "experiment freely" and "here is institutional documentation scaffolding." It must choose which participants it's primarily designing for.
Override Log purpose: craft portfolio vs. governance document vs. legitimization tool
Yael wants a career document, evidence of professional judgment. Daan wants organizational adoption evidence. Marit wants a stakeholder-facing record. Each purpose implies different prompts, different outputs, different uses after the session. Adding all three creates incoherence or requires three structurally separate versions of the same component.
Who is this program for?
The core learning arc (discover → experiment → reflect → iterate) assumes a linear progression through new capability. Daan is past that arc. Marit is blocked from entering it. Yael is on a different arc entirely (practicing judgment, not discovering capability). The program cannot be identical for all three without redesigning its progression model entirely.
Community promise vs. capability reality
The program promises peer community. Community is only valuable if the room contains people at your level. The program has no stated filtering mechanism. Heterogeneous room frustrates Daan; filtering for capability creates hierarchy and reduces the diversity of insight that benefits Yael and Marit.
High-Confidence Signals

Consensus Points

Change the Reflection Pod anchor question from "show the artifact" to "show the moment you overrode AI." All three personas either proposed this directly or voted YES. The highest-confidence single design change in the audit. Zero cost to implement.

The Override Log is the program's strongest component and is under-built. All three personas engage with it centrally and all three want it to produce something beyond the session. The consensus: more prompts, more structural depth, more deliberate output. Invest here first.

The Tiny Experiment commitment structure does not fit all participant types. All three personas have a different relationship to the commitment. Some form of differentiated commitment structure — with the specific form still contested — is a high-confidence design signal.

The program's metrics need to shift from quantity to depth. "80 experiments completed" signals quantity. All three personas are oriented around quality of judgment, depth of redesign, or institutional impact — none captured by a count. Change the metric or remove it.
